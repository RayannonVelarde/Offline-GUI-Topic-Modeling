Interviewer: Can you introduce yourself and describe your current work, study, or daily responsibilities?
Participant: I’m Elena, and I run a family restaurant with my husband, my aunt, and depending on the week, one or two cousins who swear they are “just helping out” even though they are basically part- time staff. We serve breakfast and lunch, mostly regulars, a lot of teachers, nurses from a
Participant: nearby clinic, construction crews, and families who have been coming for years. Owning a restaurant sounds to some people like one clear job, but it is really five or six jobs stitched together. I manage inventory, payroll, scheduling, customer service, social media, supplier
Participant: relationships, and sometimes I am also on the line or at the register.
Participant: My day starts before sunrise because deliveries and prep do not wait for anyone. By midmorning I may have already dealt with a late shipment, a broken appliance, two staffing texts, and a customer who wants to tell me exactly how their grandmother made chile rellenos. The work is
Participant: physical and constant. Even on “days off,” I am thinking about costs, staffing, or whether we need to change the menu price on something because eggs or produce jumped again.
Participant: What keeps me in it is that the restaurant is more than a business to me. It is one of the places where people in our neighborhood feel recognized. Regulars come in after funerals, before school events, after hospital visits, on paydays, or when they simply need familiar food
Participant: and conversation. So I think of the work as economic, but also social. We feed people, yes, but we also hold routine for them.

Interviewer: Tell me about the community or environment you grew up in and how it shaped you.
Participant: I grew up in a border environment where movement, adaptation, and bilingual communication were just normal life. People crossed for work, for school, for family, for groceries, for appointments, for celebrations. The border was never only a line. It was part of the rhythm of
Participant: everyday decision-making. That shaped me because it taught me flexibility and also taught me early that policy can interrupt ordinary life in very intimate ways.
Participant: My family was entrepreneurial in the survival sense, not the glamorous sense. My parents sold things, repaired things, bartered favors, and pieced together income from multiple directions. So I learned that work is not always linear. Sometimes stability is built from many small
Participant: moving parts rather than one secure position.
Participant: I also grew up in a family where food carried memory. Recipes were not only recipes. They were stories, migration routes, disagreements, pride, and identity. Watching women in my family cook taught me how much labor gets naturalized when it is tied to care. That has influenced how I
Participant: think about gender, family, and business to this day.

Interviewer: What have been the biggest challenges in your education or career path?
Participant: The biggest challenge in my career has been that small businesses are often romanticized in public discourse while being structurally unsupported in practice. People love the idea of local businesses, family businesses, neighborhood culture. But when rent rises, supply costs
Participant: spike, labor gets harder to retain, or regulations shift, the burden falls on owners who are already operating on thin margins.
Participant: Another challenge has been being taken seriously as a business owner rather than being read as “helpful,” “hardworking,” or “family-oriented” in ways that subtly minimize strategic skill. I know my numbers. I know my margins. I know customer flow patterns, seasonal demand, and what
Participant: operational changes actually matter. But there have been plenty of moments when suppliers or even bank representatives directed more technical language to my husband first, even though I manage most of the business systems.
Participant: The pandemic was also a major rupture. We survived, but survival came with debt, stress, and constant uncertainty. We had to redesign service, communicate safety measures, learn new digital ordering systems, and make daily decisions with incomplete information. People talk
Participant: about resilience after the fact. Living it felt more like permanent improvisation under pressure.

Interviewer: How do technology and digital tools show up in your daily life or work?
Participant: Technology entered our business more seriously in the last several years. Before, we used some basic systems but still relied heavily on paper notes, in-person habits, and what I would call embodied management—you just knew how a week was going by being present in the room. Now we use
Participant: point-of-sale data, online ordering, digital payroll systems, scheduling apps, and social media more strategically.
Participant: That has helped us in real ways. We can track what sells, communicate quickly with customers, and fill slower hours with online orders. Social media especially has changed how small restaurants build visibility. A single post from a local food account can bring in new
Participant: customers for weeks.
Participant: But technology also creates pressure to perform constantly. It is no longer enough to run the restaurant well. You are also expected to maintain an online personality, respond quickly to reviews, photograph food attractively, and adapt to platforms whose rules can change overnight.
Participant: So digital tools create opportunity, but also another layer of labor that small businesses have to absorb.

Interviewer: Can you describe a time when language, culture, or identity affected an important experience for you?
Participant: Language and identity affect almost every aspect of customer interaction in a border city. Most of our customers move between English and Spanish fluidly, and so do we. That flexibility creates warmth and efficiency. People feel understood quickly. But it also means that the
Participant: emotional texture of service can shift with language. A joke lands differently. An apology lands differently. Hospitality is not only what you do; it is how precisely you can meet someone where they are.
Participant: I remember once dealing with a supplier issue where the representative kept simplifying his language with me in a way that assumed I did not understand the business side because I had answered him in Spanish. That moment irritated me not because it was dramatic, but because it
Participant: revealed how quickly bilingualism can be misread as lesser competence instead of broader competence.
Participant: As a woman in a family business, identity also shapes expectation. People assume availability from women in service settings—emotional availability, flexibility, patience. Some of that can be a strength, but some of it becomes exploitation if you never set boundaries.

Interviewer: What kinds of support systems or relationships have helped you through difficult periods?
Participant: My support system is very much family-centered, though not in the idealized way people sometimes imagine. Family support can be loving and exhausting at the same time. We help each other, we show up, and we also argue. Running a family business means work stress can become
Participant: relationship stress very quickly. So support is there, but it is mixed with history and personality.
Participant: I also have a few close friends who run other small businesses, and those relationships are invaluable because they understand a specific kind of pressure. They know what it feels like when an appliance breaks at the worst possible time, when payroll is due, or when customer
Participant: praise does not change the fact that the month was financially rough. That kind of peer understanding is hard to replace.
Participant: My church community has also been a source of support, especially during illness and grief. Not because they solve problems magically, but because they create continuity. Meals arrive. Messages arrive. People remember important dates. Being held in community sometimes looks very
Participant: ordinary, and that ordinariness is exactly what makes it strong.

Interviewer: How do you think about mental health, stress, or burnout in your own life?
Participant: I used to think stress was just the price of being responsible. If you are an owner, a mother, a daughter, and the person people rely on, then of course you are stressed. It took me a long time to realize that normalizing constant stress is not the same as managing it.
Participant: For me, burnout shows up physically. I get headaches, my patience gets shorter, and I start resenting things that are usually meaningful to me. That is usually the point when I realize I have crossed from “busy” into “depleted.” The problem is that in food service, depletion is
Participant: easy to hide because movement can mask it. You are still doing the tasks, so from the outside you look fine.
Participant: I have gotten better at protecting small forms of restoration. Walking alone in the evening, not answering every message immediately, letting other people close the restaurant sometimes, and admitting when I need help. Those may sound simple, but for women raised to equate care
Participant: with constant service, they are not actually simple.

Interviewer: What changes have you seen in your community over the last few years?
Participant: My community has become both more connected and more precarious. There is still strong neighborhood loyalty, and people really do try to support local businesses. But household budgets are tighter, and people are more cautious with spending. You can feel it in order
Participant: sizes, in how often people eat out, in whether they split meals, in what they ask about prices.
Participant: I have also seen more public conversation about local identity and preservation. People do not want neighborhoods to lose their character. At the same time, development pressures keep pushing things toward sameness. So there is this ongoing struggle between convenience, profit,
Participant: and cultural memory.
Participant: Another major change is that younger generations are more open about mental health, work boundaries, and not wanting to inherit exhausting patterns unquestioned. I actually respect that, even if it clashes with how many of us were raised. It forces conversations older
Participant: generations often postponed indefinitely.

Interviewer: What goals or hopes do you have for the future, both personally and professionally?
Participant: Personally, I want more sustainability and less constant reaction. I want the restaurant to remain strong, but I do not want survival mode to be our permanent business model. I would like to stabilize staffing, reduce debt, and maybe eventually expand in a way that does not destroy
Participant: what makes the place personal.
Participant: I also want to make space for my own life beyond work. For years I organized everything around what the business needed. I still care deeply about it, but I am also asking who I am when I am not solving restaurant problems. That feels like an important question at this stage of my
Participant: life.
Participant: Professionally, I would love to mentor younger women who want to start businesses but have mostly seen entrepreneurship represented through industries or personalities that do not reflect their reality. There are many ways to be strategic, and many ways to build something
Participant: durable.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you think researchers should understand about people with experiences like yours?
Participant: Researchers should understand that small business owners are often treated as one economic category, but the realities vary a lot depending on industry, family structure, neighborhood, and cultural role. A family restaurant is not just a revenue stream. It can also be a social
Participant: anchor, an employer, and a keeper of local memory.
Participant: I would also say that border communities are often discussed through crisis language or political language while everyday intelligence gets overlooked. People here are highly adaptive. They navigate currency, language, policy changes, family logistics, and identity
Participant: shifts constantly. That requires analysis, not just endurance.
Participant: And finally, I think researchers should pay attention to invisible labor, especially women’s labor. There is a huge amount of coordination, emotional management, and care embedded in businesses and households that rarely appears in formal accounts, even though it is what keeps
Participant: everything running.
