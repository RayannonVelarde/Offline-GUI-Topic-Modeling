Interviewer: Can you introduce yourself and describe your current work, study, or daily responsibilities?
Participant: My name is Samira, and I work as a user experience researcher for a healthcare technology company. A lot of people hear that job title and assume my work is abstract or mostly technical, but my day is actually built around conversations. I interview patients, clinicians,
Participant: and support staff to understand where digital tools fail them, where they confuse them, and where they might actually help. Then I translate those patterns into reports, recommendations, prototypes, and arguments inside the company.
Participant: The interesting thing is that the work sits between empathy and structure. I need to be genuinely attentive to people’s experiences, but I also need to turn those experiences into something a product team can act on. So I spend a lot of time moving between open-ended
Participant: interviews and highly organized synthesis. Some days I’m leading sessions and listening closely to what people do not say directly. Other days I’m buried in transcripts, tagging themes and trying to persuade engineers that a “small usability issue” can have major effects on people
Participant: who are already vulnerable.
Participant: Outside work, I’m still very connected to immigrant community networks in Denver. I help newer arrivals navigate school enrollment, housing questions, and job applications when I can. I also spend a lot of time with my sister and her kids. So even though I work in tech, my life remains
Participant: deeply grounded in everyday social realities. That tension keeps me honest. It reminds me that elegant systems are only useful if they can survive contact with actual life.

Interviewer: Tell me about the community or environment you grew up in and how it shaped you.
Participant: I grew up in Casablanca in a family that valued education intensely, but also viewed it as tied to collective mobility rather than individual self-fulfillment. That distinction matters. Doing well in school was not only about personal achievement. It was about expanding what was
Participant: possible for the family. That gave me motivation, but it also gave achievement a certain emotional pressure.
Participant: My environment was very multilingual and layered. Arabic at home, French in formal settings, English as aspiration and later opportunity. Moving across those languages taught me early that identity is situational. You can be articulate in one room and uncertain in another, not
Participant: because your ideas changed, but because the language dynamics changed. I think that made me observant. I learned to notice tone, hierarchy, who interrupted whom, who explained too much, and who was assumed to be competent before speaking.
Participant: What shaped me most was probably living with both ambition and adaptation. My family wanted stability, but we were always recalibrating around political conditions, work opportunities, and what was realistically possible. That made me flexible, but it also meant I learned to
Participant: think ahead constantly. I am still trying to figure out when planning is wisdom and when it becomes anxiety.

Interviewer: What have been the biggest challenges in your education or career path?
Participant: The biggest challenge in my career path was learning how to present myself in ways that made sense across different cultural and professional systems. When I first moved to the United States for graduate school, I realized that being capable was not enough. You also had to
Participant: narrate your capability in the expected style: concise but confident, strategic but personable, ambitious without seeming arrogant. That performance layer took energy.
Participant: I also encountered the subtle instability of being visibly competent but still occasionally treated as provisional. Sometimes people were curious about my perspective in a way that felt meaningful, and other times it felt like I was being invited to represent all immigrants, all
Participant: multilingual users, or all non-Western assumptions in one meeting. Representation can become a burden when it is not accompanied by shared responsibility.
Participant: Another challenge has been that research roles inside companies can be respected rhetorically and sidelined practically. Everyone says they value the user voice, but deadlines and technical convenience often win. So part of my work has been learning how to advocate persuasively inside
Participant: structures that admire evidence yet still default to speed.

Interviewer: How do technology and digital tools show up in your daily life or work?
Participant: Technology is both my profession and one of the main subjects of my criticism. I work on digital health tools, so I see every day how design decisions can either reduce friction or create it. A button label, a reminder flow, a translation choice, or the order of information
Participant: on a screen can change whether someone completes an enrollment form or gives up halfway.
Participant: Personally, technology helps me maintain transnational relationships. My family life is spread across countries and cities, so video calls, group chats, and shared photos make distance emotionally survivable. I also rely on translation tools, navigation apps, cloud notes, and
Participant: collaborative documents more than I probably notice. My work would not exist without digital infrastructure.
Participant: But I am also skeptical of how neutral technology is often made to sound. Tools are shaped by assumptions about literacy, time, privacy, and confidence. If a system assumes the user has endless patience, stable broadband, and comfort disclosing information digitally, it will
Participant: exclude many people before the first error message even appears. So for me, technology is never only technical. It is social design with consequences.

Interviewer: Can you describe a time when language, culture, or identity affected an important experience for you?
Participant: One experience that shaped me happened during graduate school when a professor praised me for being “surprisingly direct” in group discussions. I know it was intended as a compliment, but it unsettled me because it revealed an expectation beneath the surface. There was an imagined
Participant: version of me—foreign, polite, maybe cautious—and my actual behavior was being measured against it.
Participant: Moments like that are usually small enough that you cannot always respond without sounding oversensitive, but they accumulate. They teach you how quickly identity gets read before your actual words are evaluated. As an immigrant woman in professional spaces, I’ve had to decide
Participant: repeatedly when to explain, when to ignore, and when to push back.
Participant: Language also affects intimacy. There are parts of myself that feel sharper in Arabic, more analytical in French, and more professionally legible in English. I do not think one language is the “real” me. I think each one gives access to different registers of the self. Researchers
Participant: sometimes talk about multilingualism like a skill set, but emotionally it is also a shifting home.

Interviewer: What kinds of support systems or relationships have helped you through difficult periods?
Participant: My support systems are a combination of family, friendship, and what I would call intentional community. My sister is probably my anchor in the United States. She understands both the practical logistics of immigrant life and the emotional subtleties. She knows when I need
Participant: advice and when I need someone to say, “Yes, that was not in your head.”
Participant: I also have close friends from graduate school and from immigrant organizing spaces. Those friendships matter because they carry shared context. There is less explaining. We can talk about visas, family obligation, professional code-switching, Islamophobia, loneliness, or
Participant: bureaucratic absurdity without starting from zero.
Participant: Therapy has also been part of my support system. I mention that because for many people it gets framed as an individual fix, but for me it has been more like a place to metabolize the tension of belonging in multiple places incompletely. That has been useful in a way that is less about
Participant: crisis and more about coherence.

Interviewer: How do you think about mental health, stress, or burnout in your own life?
Participant: I think about stress as layered. There is immediate stress, like deadlines or conflict, and then there is structural stress, which comes from always having to translate between systems, identities, and expectations. I have gotten better at noticing when I am carrying both at once.
Participant: That is usually when small tasks start to feel strangely heavy.
Participant: Burnout for me often begins cognitively. I become less curious, less patient, and more transactional. That is a warning sign because curiosity is central to my work and to how I move through the world generally. When stress narrows me like that, I know I need to slow down.
Participant: I’ve become more protective of routines that make me feel located in my body rather than only in my mind. Cooking, walking, prayer sometimes, calling family, spending time away from screens—those things sound ordinary, but they keep me from becoming a purely functional person.
Participant: I think high-achieving environments often reward function at the expense of wholeness.

Interviewer: What changes have you seen in your community over the last few years?
Participant: In Denver, I have seen both diversification and displacement intensify. There are more international communities, more cultural exchange, and more public language about inclusion. At the same time, affordability has worsened, and neighborhoods that once felt accessible are
Participant: becoming harder for working families to remain in.
Participant: I have also seen immigrant communities become more digitally connected and more surveilled at the same time. People use online spaces to share resources, warn each other, and organize quickly. But digital visibility also creates new vulnerabilities, especially for people with
Participant: precarious legal or economic situations.
Participant: Another change is that younger people are more willing to discuss identity intersectionally. They are not separating race, class, language, gender, and immigration status into neat boxes. They understand those categories as entangled. I find that encouraging, even when institutions
Participant: are slower to respond.

Interviewer: What goals or hopes do you have for the future, both personally and professionally?
Participant: Personally, I want a future that includes depth rather than constant acceleration. Professionally, I may move into more strategic research leadership, but only if I can stay connected to community-facing work. I do not want to become someone who speaks in abstractions
Participant: about users while losing contact with actual users.
Participant: I also hope to mentor younger researchers, especially multilingual and immigrant women who are often told to be grateful for access while quietly absorbing disproportionate representational labor. I want them to feel not only included, but authorized.
Participant: On a personal level, I want to preserve transnational belonging without living permanently in emotional fragmentation. I do not know exactly what that looks like yet. Maybe it means moving fluidly, maybe it means building stronger rituals where I am, maybe it means accepting that
Participant: home can be multiple and unfinished.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you think researchers should understand about people with experiences like yours?
Participant: Researchers should understand that migration is not a single event. It is an ongoing recalibration of paperwork, identity, relationships, and future planning. Even after someone appears settled from the outside, there may still be layers of uncertainty beneath that
Participant: appearance.
Participant: I would also say that multilingual people are often treated either as assets or as problems, but rarely as full subjects with complex inner lives. Language access should not be reduced to functionality. It is also about dignity, precision, and emotional range.
Participant: And finally, I think researchers need to be careful not to confuse visibility with safety. Just because communities are more publicly represented does not mean they are less vulnerable. Recognition can coexist with exclusion very easily.
