Interviewer: Can you introduce yourself and describe your current work, study, or daily responsibilities?
Participant: I’m Aisha, and I work as a housing advocate at a nonprofit that helps tenants facing eviction, unsafe conditions, or impossible rent burdens navigate a system that is often designed to move faster than their ability to respond. My job combines direct service, policy education,
Participant: documentation support, and a lot of crisis communication. On any given day I might be helping someone understand a notice, preparing materials for a workshop, speaking with an attorney, calling a landlord, or just helping a person slow down enough to think while they are in panic.
Participant: What many people do not realize is that housing work is deeply administrative and deeply emotional at the same time. A small paperwork error can have huge consequences. A missed deadline can cascade quickly. And beneath all that there is usually fear—fear of displacement,
Participant: shame, instability, impact on children, damage to credit, family conflict. So the work requires both technical accuracy and emotional steadiness.
Participant: Outside work, I have two kids, an elderly aunt nearby, and a life that is as busy as most people’s. That matters because I never approach clients as though they are uniquely disorganized or overwhelmed. Most people are trying to hold together far more than the surface
Participant: of a file reveals.

Interviewer: Tell me about the community or environment you grew up in and how it shaped you.
Participant: I grew up in a community where housing was always discussed in terms of effort and responsibility, but over time I realized those explanations were incomplete. My parents believed strongly in stability, budgeting, and not wasting money, and those values shaped me.
Participant: But I also saw how stability could be threatened by forces that had little to do with character—health problems, rent increases, layoffs, divorce, legal issues, or simply being one emergency away from falling behind.
Participant: I was raised in a Black Muslim household, and that shaped me through both discipline and perspective. There was a strong sense that dignity mattered, that service mattered, and that how systems treated people was a moral question, not just a bureaucratic one. I think that is
Participant: part of why I ended up in advocacy.
Participant: The neighborhood I grew up in also changed dramatically over time. Places that once felt ordinary and accessible became more expensive and more curated. Watching that happen taught me that displacement does not always arrive with dramatic language. Sometimes it arrives through
Participant: “improvement” that quietly excludes the people who gave a place its life.

Interviewer: What have been the biggest challenges in your education or career path?
Participant: The biggest challenge in this career is staying emotionally available without being consumed by other people’s emergencies. Housing advocacy exposes you to a lot of stories where the official record and the lived reality are miles apart. A case file might say “late payment” while the
Participant: full story involves illness, caregiving, wage theft, or domestic violence. Holding that complexity every day can be exhausting.
Participant: Another challenge is that nonprofit work often depends on short-term funding while dealing with long-term problems. The issues are structural, but the support can be temporary, piecemeal, and fragile. That can create a constant tension between what communities need and what programs are
Participant: actually resourced to do.
Participant: There is also a challenge around legitimacy. People respect legal or financial expertise more quickly than community-based advocacy expertise, even when advocates are the ones who understand the real barriers tenants face. I have had to learn how to speak across professional
Participant: cultures without surrendering the grounded perspective that made me effective in the first place.

Interviewer: How do technology and digital tools show up in your daily life or work?
Participant: Technology shows up constantly in housing work now. Applications are online, payments are online, notices are sometimes sent through portals, public resources require digital navigation, and communication moves through phones at a relentless pace. In some ways that
Participant: allows faster coordination. Documents can be sent quickly. Appointments can be scheduled more efficiently. Information can circulate widely.
Participant: But digital systems also assume stability. Stable phone numbers, stable internet, stable email access, stable mailing addresses, and enough confidence to upload forms correctly. Many of the people we work with are managing instability in exactly those areas. So technology can become
Participant: another gatekeeper if there is no support around it.
Participant: Personally, I rely on digital tools all the time, but I have become very aware that convenience is unevenly distributed. A portal can save time for one user and create panic for another. Design matters, but so does context.

Interviewer: Can you describe a time when language, culture, or identity affected an important experience for you?
Participant: Identity shapes my work constantly. As a Black Muslim woman, I am aware that some clients trust me immediately because they see me as someone who might understand being judged before speaking. Others are unsure of me at first because of their own assumptions. You learn to read
Participant: rooms quickly.
Participant: A moment that stays with me involved a tenant who had been repeatedly dismissed by property management and various offices. When we sat down, she kept apologizing for being “difficult.” The more she explained, the clearer it became that she had simply been trying to advocate for
Participant: basic repairs and was being recast as the problem. That dynamic felt familiar to me, not because our identities were identical, but because respect is often distributed unevenly.
Participant: Language matters too, even when everyone is technically speaking English. Legal and housing language can make ordinary people sound uninformed when really the terminology is just intentionally specialized. One of the most important things I do is translate institutional
Participant: speech back into human speech.

Interviewer: What kinds of support systems or relationships have helped you through difficult periods?
Participant: My support system is strong, but I have had to build it intentionally. My faith community is a major source of grounding. It offers rhythm, perspective, and people who understand service as something collective rather than individual heroism. That helps me resist the savior narrative
Participant: that nonprofit work can accidentally encourage.
Participant: I also have a few close friends in advocacy and public interest work who understand secondary stress. We can talk about the emotional leftovers of hearing hard stories all day without turning the conversation into competition over who has it worse. That balance is important.
Participant: My sister is also central to my life. She is the person who can tell when I am overextending before I say it out loud. Family support for me is not always about agreement. Sometimes it is somebody interrupting my momentum and asking whether I am disappearing into obligation again.

Interviewer: How do you think about mental health, stress, or burnout in your own life?
Participant: I think about burnout as something that thrives in professions where care and crisis overlap. In housing work, there is always another urgent case, another family at risk, another policy failure producing immediate harm. If you are not careful, urgency can become the organizing
Participant: principle of your entire nervous system.
Participant: For me, stress first shows up as hypervigilance. I check messages too often, feel responsible for outcomes beyond my control, and start carrying work stories into every quiet moment. When that happens, I know I am slipping from commitment into overidentification.
Participant: I manage that now by keeping clearer boundaries, limiting after-hours communication when possible, leaning on spiritual practices, and reminding myself that doing meaningful work does not require becoming permanently dysregulated. That lesson took time. Service and self-
Participant: abandonment can look dangerously similar if nobody teaches you the difference.

Interviewer: What changes have you seen in your community over the last few years?
Participant: The biggest changes I’ve seen in my community are rising rents, growing visibility of housing insecurity, and more public recognition that affordability is not only a problem for the very poor. More working people are struggling. More families are doubling up. More people are one
Participant: crisis away from housing instability.
Participant: I have also seen stronger tenant organizing and more willingness among younger people to question narratives that frame housing purely as an investment vehicle rather than a social necessity. That shift matters because language shapes policy possibilities.
Participant: At the same time, displacement has emotional effects that are hard to capture in statistics. When people move farther out, they lose routines, childcare networks, transit familiarity, neighborhood trust, and everyday belonging. Housing is not just shelter. It is infrastructure
Participant: for life.

Interviewer: What goals or hopes do you have for the future, both personally and professionally?
Participant: Personally, I want a future where my work remains principled but my life is not entirely organized around emergency. I want to stay in advocacy, maybe move more into policy strategy or coalition leadership, but I also want enough space for parenting, joy, and health.
Participant: I hope to help build systems where tenants do not need extraordinary persistence just to be treated fairly. That means legal reform, but it also means communication reform, administrative reform, and cultural reform around how we talk about deservingness.
Participant: On a more personal level, I want my children to grow up seeing service as powerful, but not as something that requires losing yourself. I want them to inherit commitment with boundaries, not commitment with burnout.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you think researchers should understand about people with experiences like yours?
Participant: Researchers should understand that housing instability is rarely an isolated issue. It intersects with childcare, transportation, employment, mental health, disability, immigration status, domestic safety, schooling, and debt. People do not experience those systems separately
Participant: just because agencies do.
Participant: I would also say that many tenants have already become highly skilled legal and administrative readers simply because survival requires it. They are not passive subjects waiting to be interpreted. They are analysts of the systems affecting them.
Participant: And finally, I would ask researchers to be careful with narratives of vulnerability that erase agency. People can be under pressure and still strategic, exhausted and still insightful, precarious and still deeply knowledgeable about what needs to change.
