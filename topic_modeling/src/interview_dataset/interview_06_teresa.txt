Interviewer: Can you introduce yourself and describe your current work, study, or daily responsibilities?
Participant: I’m Teresa, and I work as a nurse manager at a public hospital, which means my day is rarely what I imagined it would be when I first drove in that morning. I still identify very strongly as a nurse, but my current role sits at the intersection of patient care, staffing,
Participant: administration, policy, and crisis management. I oversee a team, deal with scheduling shortages, troubleshoot family concerns, coordinate with physicians, document compliance issues, and try to protect some humanity inside a system that often runs on urgency and
Participant: throughput.
Participant: Healthcare people understand this immediately, but from the outside I think many people imagine hospitals as highly controlled places. The reality is more fluid. Things are always shifting because people are unpredictable, illnesses are unpredictable, and staffing realities are
Participant: fragile. So a lot of leadership in a hospital is not about commanding. It is about absorbing pressure, redistributing resources, and making decisions while information is incomplete.
Participant: Outside of work, I’m also a grandmother, a daughter to an aging parent, and the default organizer in my family. So caregiving does not stop when my shift ends. Some weeks that feels meaningful. Some weeks it feels like there is no quiet room anywhere in my life. I am proud of
Participant: the work, but I do not romanticize its cost.

Interviewer: Tell me about the community or environment you grew up in and how it shaped you.
Participant: I grew up in a working-class household where practical competence mattered a lot. Nobody used words like “leadership” or “resilience,” but those things were expected. You handled what needed handling. You showed up. You learned not to fall apart publicly unless something was
Participant: truly serious. That background gave me endurance, but it also taught me to override myself for a long time.
Participant: My community also taught me the importance of trust. People noticed who was dependable, who kept confidence, and who acted one way in public and another in private. That probably influenced why I was drawn to nursing. At its best, nursing is highly skilled work built on
Participant: trust under vulnerable conditions.
Participant: I was also shaped by seeing health inequities very personally. We had family members who delayed care because of cost, confusion, pride, fear, or transportation. So the hospital was never an abstract institution to me. It was a place where social inequality showed up in very
Participant: concrete ways. That has stayed central to how I practice.

Interviewer: What have been the biggest challenges in your education or career path?
Participant: The biggest challenge in my career has been staying in healthcare long enough to build expertise while also surviving the culture of overextension that the field often normalizes. Nursing can be deeply meaningful, but it can also consume people. Especially for women, there
Participant: is often an expectation that competence equals infinite capacity.
Participant: Another challenge has been moving into management. Once you become a manager, people assume you have somehow crossed to the “administrative” side, but the truth is you are often standing in an uncomfortable middle. Staff need protection and honesty. Executives need reports and
Participant: metrics. Patients and families need care. You are translating among pressures that do not align neatly.
Participant: The pandemic intensified all of this. We lost people, we lost morale, and even those who stayed are carrying invisible residue. The public moved on faster than many healthcare workers could emotionally move on. So part of the challenge now is working in a system that wants
Participant: productivity while people are still metabolizing trauma.

Interviewer: How do technology and digital tools show up in your daily life or work?
Participant: Technology in the hospital is indispensable and frustrating. Electronic medical records, patient monitoring systems, scheduling software, messaging platforms, telehealth, barcode medication systems—none of this is optional. These tools can improve safety and continuity when
Participant: they work well.
Participant: But technology also introduces new forms of burden. Documentation can become so consuming that it competes with actual patient presence. Poorly designed systems create workarounds, and workarounds create risk. When software is built without understanding real workflow, staff
Participant: become the ones compensating.
Participant: At home, technology helps me maintain family ties, manage appointments for multiple people, and keep life moving. But after a full day of charting and electronic notifications, I sometimes feel physically tired of interfaces. There is a difference between being connected and being
Participant: saturated.

Interviewer: Can you describe a time when language, culture, or identity affected an important experience for you?
Participant: Language and identity matter in healthcare every single day. We serve many patients who are most comfortable in Spanish, and I am bilingual, so I end up bridging a lot of communication even when interpretation services are technically available. Formal interpretation is
Participant: important, but bilingual staff often become the immediate emotional bridge because we can catch nuance quickly.
Participant: A moment that stays with me involved an older patient who kept nodding during discharge instructions in English. The team assumed understanding because he was being agreeable. When I spoke with him in Spanish, it became clear he was confused and embarrassed. That kind of
Participant: situation is common. People can appear compliant while actually being lost. Language can hide vulnerability very effectively.
Participant: As a Latina in leadership, I have also had to navigate assumptions about temperament and authority. Warmth is often expected from women, especially women of color, but directness can be judged differently depending on who is using it. So I have had to learn how to be both clear
Participant: and politically strategic without diluting myself too much.

Interviewer: What kinds of support systems or relationships have helped you through difficult periods?
Participant: My support system used to be wider, but as life has gotten busier it has become more selective and more precious. My husband is steady and practical. He knows when I want to talk about work and when I need silence instead. My daughter is emotionally perceptive and often reminds me
Participant: that just because I can carry something does not mean I should carry it alone.
Participant: At work, support comes from a few long-time colleagues who have seen the field change over decades. There is comfort in not having to explain the basics of hospital fatigue to them. We can communicate in shorthand. Sometimes support is just someone saying, “That was too much,”
Participant: and knowing they mean it.
Participant: My church community has also been important, especially when my mother’s health became more complicated. People brought meals, sat with her, checked in, and took things off my plate without making a performance of it. That kind of care is easy to undervalue until you need it.

Interviewer: How do you think about mental health, stress, or burnout in your own life?
Participant: I think about burnout now less as a personal weakness and more as an occupational hazard that requires both individual awareness and structural response. For a long time I believed that if I managed myself better, worked harder, or became more efficient, I could outrun exhaustion.
Participant: That is not true in systems built on chronic understaffing.
Participant: For me, stress lives in the body first. Tight shoulders, shallow breathing, trouble sleeping, and a kind of anticipatory tension where you feel braced before anything has even happened. Emotional signs come later—short temper, numbness, detachment. Once I reach detachment, I know
Participant: I need intervention, because detachment in healthcare can become dangerous.
Participant: I have gotten better about boundaries, but boundaries in caregiving professions are complicated. There are real human needs on the other side of them. So managing mental health often means accepting that not every need can be met by me, even when I have the skill to help.
Participant: That acceptance is still difficult.

Interviewer: What changes have you seen in your community over the last few years?
Participant: The community changes I have seen are tied to inequality, aging, and trust. We are seeing more people managing chronic illness alongside economic strain, housing instability, or caregiving burdens. Those factors shape health outcomes just as much as clinical treatment does, but the
Participant: system still tends to separate them conceptually.
Participant: I’ve also noticed more openness around discussing mental health and caregiver strain, which is good. At the same time, access remains uneven. Naming the issue does not solve the issue. Many families still cannot easily obtain the care they need, especially if they are juggling
Participant: transportation, work, language barriers, and cost.
Participant: Another big change is public trust. People arrive at hospitals carrying more suspicion, more information from the internet, and sometimes more fear. Some of that is understandable. But it means relationship-building matters even more now.

Interviewer: What goals or hopes do you have for the future, both personally and professionally?
Participant: Personally, I want a future with enough spaciousness to feel like a whole person, not only a useful one. I want to remain in healthcare, but I also want to mentor younger nurses in ways that help them stay in the field without repeating every harmful pattern my generation
Participant: normalized.
Participant: Professionally, I am interested in patient safety and staff well-being at the systems level. I would like to contribute to hospital policy in a way that reflects actual floor realities, not just administrative ideals. Too many decisions are made by people who do not understand what a
Participant: shift feels like when staffing is short and the census is high.
Participant: On a personal level, I want more time with family, more rest, and maybe eventually a role that allows me to teach or guide without carrying the same acute intensity every day.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you think researchers should understand about people with experiences like yours?
Participant: Researchers should understand that healthcare workers are not only providers inside institutions. We are also patients, family members, caregivers, and community members outside those institutions. The boundaries are more porous than many studies assume.
Participant: I would also say that language access is not a decorative feature of care. It is central to safety, dignity, and informed consent. If communication is partial, the care is partial.
Participant: And finally, I think researchers need to attend to the emotional residue of caregiving work. People can keep functioning long after the system has asked too much of them. Functioning is not the same as flourishing, and it is definitely not the same as sustainability.
