Interviewer: Can you introduce yourself and describe your current work, study, or daily responsibilities?
Participant: Sure. My name is Marisa, and right now my life is split between a lot of roles that overlap with each other. During the day I work at a nonprofit clinic as a bilingual community health coordinator. That means I do a little bit of everything: outreach calls, helping families
Participant: understand insurance paperwork, coordinating transportation for appointments, and sometimes just sitting with people when the system has worn them down and they need someone to explain things in plain language. A lot of my job is not glamorous at all. It is spreadsheets,
Participant: reminders, referral lists, and trying to keep track of ten conversations at once. But a lot of it is also relational. People come back because they remember that someone listened to them and did not make them feel rushed or embarrassed.
Participant: Outside of work, I am also helping my younger brother navigate college applications and trying to support my mom, who still works long hours. So even when I clock out, I do not always feel off duty. I cook most evenings, I help with forms, I translate letters when relatives ask, and
Participant: I am usually the person in the family who gets called when something official shows up in the mail. I do not say that in a resentful way. It is just the reality of being the one who learned how to move through institutions a little more comfortably than everyone else.
Participant: What is complicated is that I am proud of all of that responsibility, but I also feel the weight of it. There are days when I feel like my actual title does not capture what my work is. It is not only health coordination. It is interpretation, advocacy, education, emotional
Participant: support, and a lot of invisible labor. I think that is true for many people in community-facing jobs. The work looks small from the outside, but it holds together entire chains of care.

Interviewer: Tell me about the community or environment you grew up in and how it shaped you.
Participant: I grew up in the South Valley area, and what shaped me most was the feeling that everyone was improvising but still trying to take care of each other. We were not wealthy, and there was always this sense that stability could change quickly. A car breaking down or hours getting cut
Participant: at work could throw off a whole month. So I grew up watching adults be very resourceful. People borrowed tools, shared rides, passed along furniture, watched each other’s kids, and traded information constantly. That taught me that community is not some abstract, sentimental thing.
Participant: It is logistical. It is who answers the phone, who tells you which office to go to, who drops off food when someone is sick.
Participant: At the same time, I grew up seeing how institutions often misunderstood neighborhoods like ours. If a school called home, it was usually because they assumed a parent was not involved, when really that parent might have been working a double shift. If someone missed an
Participant: appointment, the assumption was irresponsibility, not transportation issues or lack of childcare. So from pretty early on I became aware that people from my community were often read through a deficit lens. That stayed with me.
Participant: I also think growing up bilingual shaped me. Not in the romanticized way people sometimes talk about it, but in the practical sense of learning early that language changes power. I saw my mom become quieter in certain offices because English was not where she felt most precise, even
Participant: though she is one of the smartest people I know. Watching that taught me how much confidence and authority can be tied to language, not just knowledge.

Interviewer: What have been the biggest challenges in your education or career path?
Participant: The biggest challenge in my education was learning how to decode institutions that seemed written for people who already belonged there. I was a strong student, but I did not know the hidden rules. I did not know how office hours worked, how internships mattered, how financial
Participant: aid could shift from semester to semester, or how networking was supposed to happen when you were also commuting and working. I spent a lot of time feeling like everyone else had received instructions that I somehow missed.
Participant: Money was part of it too. There were semesters where I was balancing coursework with two jobs, and no matter how disciplined you are, exhaustion catches up to you. I remember writing papers late at night after work and then waking up early for class feeling like my brain was moving
Participant: through mud. There is a way people talk about resilience that leaves out how inefficient poverty makes everything. It takes longer to do well when every step requires extra labor.
Participant: In my career, the challenge has been that helping professions often expect deep commitment while paying modestly. People tell you the work is meaningful, and it is, but meaning does not pay rent. So I have had to navigate that tension between wanting to stay in community-based
Participant: work and needing a sustainable future. I know I am not alone in that. A lot of talented people leave because the structure asks for too much sacrifice.

Interviewer: How do technology and digital tools show up in your daily life or work?
Participant: Technology is everywhere in my job, but not always in a way that feels empowering. On one hand, digital tools make coordination possible. We use shared spreadsheets, intake systems, translation apps in emergencies, telehealth portals, scheduling software, and text reminder
Participant: platforms. Without those systems, we would lose track of people. They help us document care, follow up faster, and keep some continuity.
Participant: But the other side is that technology often assumes a level of access and digital comfort that many of our clients do not have. Someone might technically have a phone but no reliable data plan. Or they may share one device across a household. Or they may receive a portal message in
Participant: English full of medical language that is technically translated but not actually understandable. So a lot of my work is bridging what people call access and what I would call usable access. Those are very different things.
Participant: Personally, I rely on technology a lot too. My calendar runs my life. I keep voice notes for things I do not want to forget. I text with family members all day. But I also feel digital fatigue. By the end of some workdays, I am tired of screens telling me what needs attention.
Participant: Technology helps me stay organized, but it also keeps me tethered to obligation.

Interviewer: Can you describe a time when language, culture, or identity affected an important experience for you?
Participant: A moment that stands out is from my first year of college when I was helping my parents at an orientation event. There was a session for families, and most of the materials were technically available in Spanish, but the room was still structured in a way that made Spanish-speaking
Participant: parents feel peripheral. Questions were answered quickly in English first, and then translated in summary form. It sounds minor, but the effect was clear. Parents who were already unsure whether they belonged there became even less likely to speak.
Participant: I remember my mom asking me afterward whether she had missed something important, and I felt angry because she had not missed anything intellectually. The structure had simply signaled that the “real” conversation happened somewhere slightly out of reach. That experience
Participant: sharpened something for me. Inclusion is not just about providing a translated handout. It is about whether people can participate with dignity and in real time.
Participant: Identity shows up in subtler ways too. As a Latina professional, I often notice that people assume I am more approachable but not necessarily more authoritative. Sometimes that works in my favor because people open up. But sometimes I have to be very deliberate about setting
Participant: boundaries and demonstrating expertise, especially in settings where warmth gets mistaken for lack of rigor.

Interviewer: What kinds of support systems or relationships have helped you through difficult periods?
Participant: My family has been my first support system, even when all of us were stretched thin. We are not the kind of family that sits in a circle and says, “Let’s process our emotions.” Support is usually practical. Someone drops off groceries, watches a child, sends a reminder text, or
Participant: says, “I can take that shift for you.” There is a lot of care embedded in action. I have learned to read love through consistency more than through language.
Participant: Outside my family, I have been helped by mentors, especially women who were a few years ahead of me. They showed me how to ask for things without apologizing, how to leave spaces that treated me as permanently grateful, and how to think about a career as something I could shape
Participant: rather than simply survive. That mattered because when you are first-generation in a lot of spaces, you can internalize the idea that opportunity means accepting whatever terms are offered.
Participant: I also have a close group of friends from college and work who understand the emotional vocabulary of service-oriented jobs. We can talk about compassion fatigue, bureaucracy, guilt, and burnout without over-explaining. That kind of peer support matters because some experiences
Participant: are hard to translate to people who have never had to hold crisis and paperwork in the same hand.

Interviewer: How do you think about mental health, stress, or burnout in your own life?
Participant: I think about stress as something that accumulates quietly before it announces itself. For me, burnout does not usually look dramatic. It looks like irritability, forgetfulness, answering messages more slowly, and feeling emotionally flat when I would normally feel present. Because
Participant: my job involves caring for others, I sometimes miss my own warning signs. You can tell yourself you are just being responsible when actually you are depleted.
Participant: Mental health was not something openly discussed in my family when I was younger. There was love, but the language was limited. Therapy was seen as something either unnecessary or inaccessible. Over time I have tried to unlearn that. I have gone to therapy on and off, and it
Participant: helped me understand that being strong is not the same as being endlessly available.
Participant: I still struggle with guilt around rest. I think many people from working-class backgrounds do. Rest can feel undeserved when there is always something unfinished. So part of my mental health work has been learning that boundaries are not selfish. They are what allow care to continue
Participant: without turning into self-erasure.

Interviewer: What changes have you seen in your community over the last few years?
Participant: In the last few years I have seen my community become more visible and more pressured at the same time. There are more conversations about equity, health access, food systems, and bilingual services, which is encouraging. There are also more young people organizing and
Participant: speaking with confidence about things older generations often had to navigate more quietly. That gives me hope.
Participant: At the same time, cost of living has increased, and many families feel squeezed in ways that are very concrete. Rent is higher, groceries are higher, and even when jobs are technically available, they are not always stable or adequately paid. So you hear a lot of language about
Participant: opportunity alongside a lot of lived insecurity. Both are true.
Participant: I also think the pandemic changed social trust. Some people became more connected and mutual- aid oriented, but others became more isolated. In community work, I have noticed that rebuilding trust takes time. People are tired of systems that appear supportive in theory and
Participant: inconsistent in practice.

Interviewer: What goals or hopes do you have for the future, both personally and professionally?
Participant: Personally, I want a life that feels sustainable, not just impressive on paper. I want to keep doing meaningful work, but I also want financial stability, time for relationships, and enough margin in my life that every small emergency does not feel catastrophic. Professionally, I am
Participant: thinking about graduate school in public health or health policy, but only if I can do it in a way that does not pull me too far from community-based practice.
Participant: I want to be in roles where decisions get made, not only roles where I am cleaning up the consequences of other people’s decisions. Right now I am often the person translating policy into reality for families. Eventually I want to help shape policy with a stronger understanding
Participant: of what implementation actually feels like on the ground.
Participant: More broadly, I hope to build a future where care work is taken seriously. Not celebrated abstractly, but materially supported. I want institutions to value the people who make systems legible and humane.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you think researchers should understand about people with experiences like yours?
Participant: I would want researchers to understand that people’s lives are more integrated than the categories used in most reports. Health is tied to transportation, work schedules, language access, family obligations, housing, stress, immigration history, and trust. People do not
Participant: experience those as separate variables. They experience them all at once.
Participant: I would also want researchers to be careful about framing communities only through need. Need is real, but so are skill, humor, intelligence, memory, and strategy. If you only study hardship, you can miss the forms of expertise people build while navigating hardship.
Participant: And finally, I would say that being asked questions can feel extractive if people never see what comes from the research. So if researchers want honesty, they should also think about accountability. Who benefits from the knowledge, and who gets it back in usable form?
