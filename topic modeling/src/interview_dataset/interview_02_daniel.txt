Interviewer: Can you introduce yourself and describe your current work, study, or daily responsibilities?
Participant: I’m Daniel, and most people around here know me first as the history teacher at the high school and second as the soccer coach who is always hauling cones and water coolers around in the back of an old truck. My days start early because I teach first period, and they end late during the
Participant: season because practice, games, and parent conversations all spill beyond the contract hours. In between, I grade, plan, answer student emails, fill out district paperwork, and do the kind of pastoral work that never appears in the job description. Students stop by to ask about
Participant: scholarships, jobs, family issues, and sometimes just because they need a calm room for ten minutes.
Participant: I also help my parents on weekends because they still live on family land outside town and there is always something that needs doing, whether it is fixing fencing, checking irrigation lines, or helping move equipment. So my life is not divided neatly between professional and
Participant: personal. Teaching in a small place means your students work at the grocery store, their parents are at the hardware store, and your own family history overlaps with theirs. There is a lot of accountability in that, but also a lot of intimacy.
Participant: I like that my work feels rooted. I am not just delivering content from a textbook. I am helping young people imagine futures from a place where futures are often discussed in narrow terms. Sometimes that feels energizing, and sometimes it feels heavy, because you know the
Participant: opportunities available to them are shaped by broader systems they did not choose.

Interviewer: Tell me about the community or environment you grew up in and how it shaped you.
Participant: I grew up in this area, and rural life taught me that distance changes everything. Distance changes access to school programs, healthcare, groceries, internet quality, extracurriculars, and jobs. It also changes how people rely on each other. When things are farther apart,
Participant: relationships do more work. If someone gets stranded, you call a neighbor. If a family has a medical appointment in the city, somebody’s cousin may end up driving them. So I learned pretty early that self-sufficiency is partly a myth. Even the people who sound most independent are
Participant: relying on a web of others.
Participant: At the same time, rural communities get stereotyped in ways that flatten them. People assume either backwardness or simplicity, and neither one captures the complexity I grew up with. There is deep knowledge here—about land, weather, water, local history, mechanics, animals, and
Participant: survival—but that knowledge does not always get treated as legitimate if it is not wrapped in institutional language.
Participant: What shaped me most was probably that tension between pride and limitation. I love where I’m from. I also know what it means to watch talented people leave because staying can feel like shrinking your options. That contradiction has followed me into adulthood and into teaching.

Interviewer: What have been the biggest challenges in your education or career path?
Participant: My career challenge has been fighting the idea that returning home meant settling for less. When I told people I wanted to teach here, some reacted like I was abandoning ambition. But I came back very intentionally. The challenge was making peace with the fact that some forms of
Participant: prestige are geographically coded. A lot of people assume meaningful professional growth only happens in larger cities or more visible institutions.
Participant: Inside the work itself, education has become more administratively heavy every year. Teachers are expected to be instructors, counselors, behavior managers, data analysts, communication specialists, and sometimes surrogate social workers. The challenge is that those needs are
Participant: real, but the system responds by layering more tasks onto already stretched people. There are weeks when the emotional labor of teaching feels bigger than the actual lesson planning.
Participant: Another challenge is that rural schools are constantly trying to do more with less. We don’t always have enough staff, enough course variety, or enough mental health support. So teachers become stopgaps. We improvise. We write recommendation letters late at night. We coach. We
Participant: mentor. We fill gaps because the students deserve it, but that also means exhaustion can become normalized.

Interviewer: How do technology and digital tools show up in your daily life or work?
Participant: Technology is one of the clearest examples of uneven development in rural education. In theory, the district has become more modern. Students have devices, grades are online, assignments are posted digitally, and many resources are cloud-based. On paper, that sounds like progress. In
Participant: practice, it depends on whether students have reliable internet at home, a quiet place to work, and parents who can navigate the systems.
Participant: During remote learning, those inequalities became impossible to ignore. Some students were doing assignments on weak phone connections in parked cars near public buildings. Others were sharing a single device with siblings. That period made it obvious that access is not just
Participant: about distributing hardware. It is about infrastructure and context.
Participant: Personally, I use technology constantly now. I build lesson slides, communicate through apps, review film for the team, and track student progress digitally. But I also miss the slower, more embodied parts of teaching. Sometimes I think digital tools increase efficiency while also
Participant: increasing surveillance and expectation. Because everything can be updated instantly, people start expecting instant availability.

Interviewer: Can you describe a time when language, culture, or identity affected an important experience for you?
Participant: Language and identity show up a lot in this region because history is layered here. You have families who have been in the area for generations, Native communities nearby, newer arrivals, and multiple ways of relating to land and belonging. In school, I’ve seen students feel
Participant: powerful when local history includes them and invisible when it does not.
Participant: One memory I keep returning to is teaching a unit where a student told me it was the first time he had seen his family’s kind of history treated like something more than a footnote. He did not say it dramatically, but it stayed with me because curriculum sends messages about who
Participant: counts. If local identity only appears as scenery, students absorb that.
Participant: Personally, being from here has also affected how I am read when I go elsewhere. People sometimes hear my background and assume a kind of provincialism. They do not see the nuance or the cultural crossings that rural borderlands hold. So identity, for me, is often about
Participant: correcting the story others tell about places like this.

Interviewer: What kinds of support systems or relationships have helped you through difficult periods?
Participant: My support systems have changed over time, but they are still rooted in relationships that are durable rather than flashy. My parents are steady. They are not always verbally expressive, but if I need help, they are there. My sister lives in Santa Fe now, and she is the person I call
Participant: when I need perspective instead of just reassurance. She asks the hard questions.
Participant: In town, my closest support probably comes from a few other teachers and coaches who understand how porous the job can be. We talk after games, share rides to tournaments, split responsibilities, and occasionally vent in the parking lot after difficult days. That kind of
Participant: everyday solidarity matters more than people realize. It keeps frustration from turning into isolation.
Participant: I’ve also been supported by former teachers of mine who are now colleagues. There is something grounding about working alongside people who knew you as a teenager and still believe you belong in the work. It creates continuity, which can be rare in stressful professions.

Interviewer: How do you think about mental health, stress, or burnout in your own life?
Participant: I think stress became easier for me to recognize only after I had been carrying too much for a long time. For years I treated fatigue like background noise. I assumed being tired, mentally overloaded, and emotionally thin was simply what adulthood felt like. It took a couple of
Participant: really rough semesters for me to realize I was functioning, but not well.
Participant: For me, burnout looks like cynicism first. That is when I know I need to pay attention. If I start seeing students as problems to manage rather than people to teach, then I have gone too far. The danger in education is that dedication can hide unhealthy patterns. Staying late every
Participant: day looks noble until it starts hollowing you out.
Participant: I try to manage stress better now. I run, I spend time outside, and I’ve gotten more comfortable saying no to extra responsibilities sometimes. But I still think a lot of workplaces individualize burnout instead of asking what conditions produce it. Teachers are
Participant: constantly given wellness advice in systems that remain structurally exhausting.

Interviewer: What changes have you seen in your community over the last few years?
Participant: The biggest changes I’ve seen are demographic, economic, and emotional. More families are juggling unstable work, commuting farther, or moving between households. Some students carry adult responsibilities earlier than they should. There is also more visible anxiety among young
Participant: people. They are dealing with social media, economic uncertainty, academic pressure, and a sense that the future is expensive.
Participant: At the same time, I’ve seen more openness around mental health and more willingness among students to talk about identity, fairness, and what they want from institutions. They are often more articulate than adults give them credit for. They can name contradictions clearly. They
Participant: know when a school says one thing and does another.
Participant: I’ve also seen stronger community organizing in response to scarcity. When programs are threatened, people rally. When a family loses housing or faces illness, people mobilize. So the community has become both more strained and, in some moments, more consciously interconnected.

Interviewer: What goals or hopes do you have for the future, both personally and professionally?
Participant: Personally, I want to keep building a life that feels local but not closed. I want to remain in education, maybe eventually move into curriculum leadership or community-based educational planning, but I do not want to lose the direct contact with students that gives the work
Participant: meaning.
Participant: I also hope to create more opportunities here so that leaving is a choice rather than an obligation. I want students to feel they can stay, go, return, or move between those options without seeing one path as automatically superior. That probably sounds idealistic, but I think
Participant: aspirations are shaped by what communities normalize.
Participant: On a personal level, I want more balance. I want a family eventually, and I do not want to build that on constant depletion. So part of my future goal is simply to remain useful without being consumed by usefulness.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you think researchers should understand about people with experiences like yours?
Participant: Researchers should understand that rural communities are not empty spaces between “real” centers of activity. They are places with memory, intelligence, and social infrastructure, even when formal infrastructure is weak. If you only measure what is missing, you will miss what is
Participant: being built informally all the time.
Participant: I’d also say that young people here are often balancing attachment and departure. They love their communities and also know the constraints. That emotional complexity matters. A lot of policy language assumes mobility is the obvious answer, but moving can involve grief, guilt,
Participant: and loss of reciprocity.
Participant: And finally, I would want researchers to treat local knowledge as knowledge, not just anecdote. People here understand their conditions with a sophistication that may not sound academic, but it is analytical all the same.
