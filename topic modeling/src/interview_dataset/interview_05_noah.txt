Interviewer: Can you introduce yourself and describe your current work, study, or daily responsibilities?
Participant: I’m Noah, and right now I live in that strange space where my life is both highly scheduled and still somehow unstable. I’m in a graduate program focused on environmental policy, and I also work with a local climate justice coalition. So my days move between seminars, research,
Participant: organizing meetings, community events, and a lot of reading that follows me into the evening whether I want it to or not.
Participant: The academic part of my life involves policy analysis, writing, and trying to understand how environmental decision-making actually works once you get past the public language. The organizing part is much more relational. It is coalition building, facilitating meetings,
Participant: canvassing sometimes, talking with residents about transit, air quality, housing, and energy burdens, and trying to connect environmental issues to the things people are already dealing with every day. That part matters to me because climate discourse can become very abstract very
Participant: quickly.
Participant: I also work part-time in a campus office to help cover expenses, so there is always a financial layer underneath the intellectual one. Graduate school can look very prestigious from the outside while still being materially fragile for a lot of students. I think people forget that
Participant: because the setting looks polished.

Interviewer: Tell me about the community or environment you grew up in and how it shaped you.
Participant: I grew up in suburban Colorado, but not in a particularly political household. What shaped me was more the contradiction between the way the region marketed itself and what I observed over time. I grew up with a lot of language about nature, outdoor identity, and environmental
Participant: appreciation. At the same time, I saw sprawl, traffic, wildfire smoke, water anxiety, and huge inequalities in who got to enjoy “healthy” environments versus who lived near industrial corridors or long commutes.
Participant: My parents were both public sector workers, so I grew up with some faith in institutions, but also with enough exposure to bureaucracy to know that good intentions and good outcomes are not the same. Dinner table conversations were often about systems that sounded reasonable on paper
Participant: and frustrating in practice. I think that shaped how I understand policy now. I do not see it as a purely technical field. I see it as a field of translation, power, and tradeoffs.
Participant: I was also shaped by digital culture in a big way. A lot of my political education happened online at first. That had benefits, but it also meant I had to learn later that information and action are not identical. Reading threads is not the same as building trust with people in a
Participant: room.

Interviewer: What have been the biggest challenges in your education or career path?
Participant: One challenge has been resisting the pressure to perform certainty. In graduate school and organizing spaces, there are both explicit and implicit expectations about how articulate, principled, and informed you should sound. That can make it hard to admit confusion, especially
Participant: when the issues are genuinely complex. I have had to learn that clarity is not the same as pretending to know everything.
Participant: Financial precarity has been another challenge. There is a weird class dimension in policy and advocacy spaces where unpaid experience, low-paid internships, and constant availability are normalized. That favors people with support systems or safety nets. If you are juggling rent,
Participant: work, and school, you cannot always be the most visible person in the room, even if you care deeply and think carefully.
Participant: I also struggle with the pace of institutional change. Once you understand the scale of environmental problems, it can be hard not to feel urgency all the time. But urgency without strategy becomes panic, and panic is hard to sustain collectively. Learning how to stay
Participant: committed without burning myself out has been one of the main challenges of this path.

Interviewer: How do technology and digital tools show up in your daily life or work?
Participant: Technology is central to how organizing happens now, for better and worse. We use messaging platforms, shared documents, sign-up forms, digital mapping tools, event platforms, and social media constantly. Those tools make coordination across large groups much easier. You can
Participant: mobilize people quickly, circulate information, and keep projects moving even when everyone is dispersed.
Participant: But technology also compresses time. There is always another update, another article, another crisis, another request for attention. It can create the illusion that responsiveness equals effectiveness. Sometimes the most important work is slow relationship-building, and digital
Participant: platforms are not naturally structured to reward that.
Participant: Personally, I use technology for almost everything—classwork, political information, scheduling, music, transit, reading. But I have become more suspicious of the way digital life fragments attention. If I am not careful, I can spend an entire day processing issues without
Participant: actually grounding myself in place, body, or community. That is not sustainable activism. It is just overstimulation with moral language attached.

Interviewer: Can you describe a time when language, culture, or identity affected an important experience for you?
Participant: Identity affected me a lot when I began working in environmental justice spaces and realized how dominated mainstream environmentalism had often been by a very narrow demographic and cultural style. I’m a white guy, so I move through a lot of spaces with assumptions of
Participant: legitimacy that are not given equally to others. Becoming aware of that changed how I listen and how I understand my role.
Participant: A particular moment that stays with me was being in a meeting where residents from a lower- income neighborhood were talking about traffic pollution, utility bills, and asthma, while some outside advocates kept trying to redirect the conversation to broad emissions targets. The
Participant: disconnect was stark. It showed me that language can flatten lived urgency if it is not accountable to people’s actual priorities.
Participant: So identity for me has involved learning how not to treat analysis as ownership. Just because you can explain a framework does not mean you are the person who should lead every conversation. That has been an important lesson.

Interviewer: What kinds of support systems or relationships have helped you through difficult periods?
Participant: My support system is a mix of old friends, movement friends, and a small but important family network. My parents are supportive even when they do not always fully understand what I am working on. They worry about financial stability, which honestly is fair. Their support tends
Participant: to be practical and gentle rather than ideological.
Participant: The people who understand me best right now are probably a few close friends in organizing and graduate school. We share notes, debrief events, remind each other to eat, and sometimes step in when one of us is carrying too much. That mutual accountability matters because activist
Participant: spaces can drift into martyrdom if nobody interrupts the pattern.
Participant: I have also benefited from mentors who model steadiness instead of constant intensity. The best organizers I know are not the loudest people. They are the ones who remain trustworthy over time. Seeing that has changed what kind of person I want to become in this work.

Interviewer: How do you think about mental health, stress, or burnout in your own life?
Participant: Stress is almost ambient in climate work because the subject itself contains scale, grief, urgency, and uncertainty. It is easy to become psychologically flooded if you do not develop a way to metabolize that. For me, burnout starts when everything feels equally important and I
Participant: lose the ability to prioritize. Then I become scattered, guilty, and weirdly numb at the same time.
Participant: I have also had periods where activism became too tied to identity, and that made rest feel like betrayal. If you think your worth depends on constant engagement, then pausing can feel morally suspect. I think a lot of younger organizers struggle with that. We have inherited
Participant: crises and also inherited highly visible cultures of performance around responding to them.
Participant: What helps me now is structure. Sleep, exercise, phone-free time, actual meals, being outdoors without turning it into content, and having friends who can tell when I am spiraling into abstraction. I still care intensely, but I’m trying to understand sustainability as political,
Participant: not just personal.

Interviewer: What changes have you seen in your community over the last few years?
Participant: The community changes I notice most are the widening gaps between public values and material conditions. There is a lot of visible support for sustainability, inclusion, and wellness in places like Boulder, but affordability keeps pushing many workers farther away, and that has
Participant: environmental and social consequences. You cannot claim climate consciousness while reproducing housing patterns that generate long commutes and displacement.
Participant: I have also seen more young people connecting climate issues to housing, labor, public health, and racial justice. That is a positive shift. The language is broader now, and the analysis is better in many spaces than it was even a few years ago.
Participant: At the same time, I think people are exhausted. Wildfire smoke, heat, cost of living, political polarization, and information overload have changed the emotional atmosphere. People want solutions, but many are also running low on bandwidth.

Interviewer: What goals or hopes do you have for the future, both personally and professionally?
Participant: Personally, I want to build a life where my politics and my daily practices are aligned enough that I am not constantly living in cognitive dissonance. That does not mean purity. I do not believe in purity. But I do want integrity.
Participant: Professionally, I’m interested in community-centered policy work, maybe at the city or regional level, where climate planning connects directly with transit, housing, and public participation. I want to be close enough to implementation that ideas meet reality.
Participant: I also want ordinary forms of happiness. Stable relationships, a livable home, time to cook, time outside, maybe children someday. I do not want commitment to justice to mean permanent self-abandonment. I think building livable futures should include the right to live one.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you think researchers should understand about people with experiences like yours?
Participant: Researchers should understand that people are often more ready to engage environmental issues when those issues are connected to immediate lived conditions rather than framed as distant moral tests. Air quality, rent, transit, heat, utility costs—those are climate conversations
Participant: too.
Participant: I would also say that many younger people are carrying chronic anticipatory stress about the future. That affects how they plan careers, relationships, and where to live. It is not always visible, but it shapes decision-making.
Participant: And finally, I think researchers should be cautious about extractive storytelling in activist communities. People get interviewed, mapped, and surveyed repeatedly. If research does not feed back into usable strategy or material change, people notice.
